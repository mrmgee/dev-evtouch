Giant Kelp

Redwood of the algae world, this brownish gold, fast-growing plant can reach 100 feet (30 meters) in height. The plant’s flattened blade is where cells absorb water and carbon dioxide from the sea and, through photosynthesis, convert the ingredients into oxygen and food compounds.

Each giant kelp is really two plants. The large, leafy sporophyte plants that sway gently in the sea currents produce spores; smaller plants that sprout at the plant’s feet, called gametophyte, produce eggs or sperm that create a new generation of giant kelp.

Full-grown giant kelp may live for seven years or more. They grow in cold-water coastal areas, primarily along the west coasts of North and South America.
